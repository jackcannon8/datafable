Policy Brief by Jack Cannon

Executive Summary

We often associate wealthier countries with being more successful. But how well does GDP per capita reflect national success? This brief uses data from the 2024 World Happiness Report to show that GDP only improves happiness to a pointâ€”after which, non-material factors like social support and personal freedoms become more important.

Current Landscape

GDP per capita is a traditional marker of national success, but the World Happiness Report reveals diminishing returns in happiness as GDP grows. Once basic needs are met, more income has limited impact.

Importance of Non-Material Factors

In wealthier countries, happiness is better predicted by things like social support, freedom, and life expectancy.

Distributional Insights

Countries with lower GDPs can still have high happiness scores, highlighting the impact of governance, health, and social policy.

Policy Recommendations

  • Invest in social support systems, personal freedoms, and public health infrastructure.
  • Empower individuals through community and autonomy-focused initiatives.

Conclusion

GDP is just one part of a much larger happiness story. Prioritizing health, freedom, and connection may prove far more impactful in shaping truly successful nations.
